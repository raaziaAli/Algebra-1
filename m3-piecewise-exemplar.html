Electricity Tariff (kWh pricing tiers) — Saudi Electricity Company (SEC)
The Saudi Electricity Company charges different rates per kilowatt-hour depending on how much electricity a household uses each month. For the first 6,000 kWh the rate is 0.18 SAR/kWh. From 6,001 to 10,000 kWh the rate rises to 0.30 SAR/kWh. Above 10,000 kWh the rate rises further to 0.36 SAR/kWh.

A single formula cannot model this because the rule changes at specific boundary values — 6,000 and 10,000 kWh. Each interval requires its own equation, which is exactly what a piecewise function provides.

3.3 — Real-World Interpretation
12 pts✓ PR — 12/12
For each piece and boundary, explain what it means in plain English.
Piece 1 — f(x) = 0.18x   when   0 ≤ x ≤ 6000
For households consuming up to 6,000 kWh per month, the rate is 0.18 SAR/kWh — the subsidised tier for light and moderate users. A family using 3,000 kWh pays SAR 540. The slope of 0.18 means every extra kWh adds just 18 halala to the bill — a gentle, affordable rate designed to cover basic household needs.
Piece 2 — f(x) = 0.30x − 720   when   6000 < x ≤ 10000
For households consuming between 6,001 and 10,000 kWh, the rate rises to 0.30 SAR/kWh — 67% higher than Piece 1. The constant −720 is not arbitrary: it ensures the bill is exactly SAR 1,080 at x = 6,000, so there is no sudden jump. The steeper slope reflects a policy of discouraging higher consumption.
Piece 3 — f(x) = 0.36x − 1320   when   x > 10000
For very high consumers using over 10,000 kWh, the premium rate of 0.36 SAR/kWh applies. The constant −1,320 preserves continuity at x = 10,000. This tier covers the marginal cost of peak electricity generation and is designed to price heavy users out of excessive consumption.
This function has NO jump discontinuities — it is continuous at both boundaries. The constants −720 and −1,320 in Pieces 2 and 3 were calculated specifically to prevent jumps. In practical terms, a customer crossing from Tier 1 to Tier 2 by a single kWh does not suddenly face a higher bill on all their previous consumption — the transition is smooth.

4.1 — Section 1: Introduction
8 pts✓ PR — 8/8
Introduce your scenario. Define x and f(x). Explain why piecewise functions are the right model. (Minimum 5 sentences.)
Electricity pricing in Saudi Arabia is structured as a tiered tariff system operated by the Saudi Electricity Company (SEC). Rather than charging a flat rate per kilowatt-hour, the SEC applies progressively higher rates as monthly consumption increases — a policy designed to encourage energy conservation while ensuring affordable electricity for low-income households.

In this project, I model the monthly electricity bill as a piecewise function. The independent variable x represents the total electricity consumed in a month, measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh). The dependent variable f(x) represents the total monthly bill in Saudi Riyals (SAR).

A piecewise function is the correct mathematical model because the rate per kWh is not constant — it changes at specific consumption thresholds of 6,000 and 10,000 kWh. A single linear formula cannot capture this behaviour: it would either overcharge light users or undercharge heavy users. Only a piecewise function, which applies a different rule for each interval of x, accurately represents the real pricing structure.
💡
Examiner Note: This introduction covers all three required elements: (1) real-world context with a specific company, (2) x and f(x) defined with units, and (3) a precise argument for why piecewise is necessary. The sentence "it would either overcharge light users or undercharge heavy users" is particularly strong — it shows genuine understanding rather than memorised definitions.
4.2 — Section 3: Real-World Interpretation Narrative
12 pts✓ PR — 12/12
Write your full interpretation narrative. Cover every piece, every boundary, and the continuity discussion. (Minimum 8 sentences.)
The piecewise function f(x) models the monthly electricity bill (SAR) as a function of consumption x (kWh) and has three pieces corresponding to three pricing tiers.

The first piece, f(x) = 0.18x for 0 ≤ x ≤ 6,000 kWh, represents the subsidised tier for light users. At SAR 0.18 per kWh, a household using 3,000 kWh pays SAR 540 — an affordable rate covering typical household needs. The slope of 0.18 is the marginal cost per kWh in this tier.

The second piece, f(x) = 0.30x − 720 for 6,001 to 10,000 kWh, applies a higher rate of SAR 0.30 per kWh. The constant −720 ensures continuity: at exactly x = 6,000, this piece gives 0.30(6000) − 720 = 1,080 SAR — exactly the same as Piece 1. A customer crossing into Tier 2 faces no sudden increase in their entire bill; only kWh beyond 6,000 are priced at the higher rate.

The third piece, f(x) = 0.36x − 1,320 for x > 10,000, is the premium tier designed to price excessive consumption. The constant −1,320 again ensures continuity at x = 10,000. A household using 12,000 kWh pays SAR 3,000 — significantly higher per unit than Tier 1 users.

The function is continuous at both boundaries (x = 6,000 and x = 10,000), meaning there are no sudden jumps in the bill. This is a fair design: a customer who crosses a tier boundary by a single kWh does not face a disproportionate penalty.
💡
Examiner Note: This narrative covers every piece with specific values, every boundary with continuity verification, and uses precise language throughout. The explanation of the −720 constant is the distinguishing feature of a Proficient answer — most Developing answers ignore it or just say "to make it continuous" without explaining how.

Day 5 — Absolute Value Extension

Connect piecewise functions to absolute value and prepare for Math Talk.

5.1 — Absolute Value as a Piecewise Function
8 pts✓ PR — 8/8
Write f(x) = |x| as a piecewise function. Explain why |x| is always non-negative.
f(x) = {
   x     if   x ≥ 0
  −x     if   x < 0
// When x is negative, −x is positive
When x ≥ 0, Piece 1 applies: f(x) = x, which is already non-negative. ✓

When x < 0, Piece 2 applies: f(x) = −x. Since x is negative, −x is positive. For example, if x = −5, then f(−5) = −(−5) = 5 > 0. ✓

Therefore for every possible value of x, f(x) = |x| is either positive (when x ≠ 0) or zero (when x = 0). It can never be negative because both pieces produce non-negative outputs.
💡
Examiner Note: The most common errors are writing only one piece, using x > 0 instead of x ≥ 0 (the value at x = 0 matters!), or not explaining why −x is positive when x < 0. The worked numerical example (x = −5) is excellent practice for the Math Talk.

f(x) = 0.09|x − 6000| + 1080
The vertex is at (6000, 1080). In the electricity tariff context, this represents the first pricing boundary: a household consuming exactly 6,000 kWh per month pays SAR 1,080. This is the minimum point where the pricing structure changes from the subsidised tier to the higher tier.

The value a = 0.09 is half the difference between the slopes of Piece 2 (0.30) and Piece 1 (0.18), i.e. (0.30 − 0.18) ÷ 2 = 0.06 — close to 0.09, creating a symmetric absolute value approximation centred at the Tier 1/Tier 2 boundary.

The explanation of how a relates to the slope difference shows mathematical depth beyond what is required — this is exactly the kind of insight that earns full marks. For your own scenario, choose h and k at a meaningful boundary point from your data.

Sample answers to the three Math Talk questions your teacher will ask.
“Walk me through your piecewise function. What does each piece mean?”
My function f(x) models the monthly electricity bill in SAR, where x is consumption in kWh. It has three pieces. Piece 1, f(x) = 0.18x, covers 0 to 6,000 kWh — the subsidised tier at 18 halala per kWh. Piece 2, f(x) = 0.30x − 720, covers 6,001 to 10,000 kWh at 30 halala per kWh. Piece 3, f(x) = 0.36x − 1,320, covers above 10,000 kWh at the premium rate of 36 halala per kWh. The function is continuous at both boundaries — no sudden jumps in the bill when crossing a tier.
“Is your function continuous? How do you know — show me the calculation.”
Yes, continuous at both boundaries. At x = 6,000: Piece 1 gives 0.18 × 6000 = 1080, Piece 2 gives 0.30 × 6000 − 720 = 1800 − 720 = 1080. Both equal 1080 — no jump. ✅
At x = 10,000: Piece 2 gives 0.30 × 10000 − 720 = 2280, Piece 3 gives 0.36 × 10000 − 1320 = 3600 − 1320 = 2280. Both equal 2280 — no jump. ✅
“Give me a specific x-value and tell me which piece you use and what f(x) equals.”
For x = 8,000 kWh: since 6000 < 8000 ≤ 10000, I use Piece 2. f(8000) = 0.30 × 8000 − 720 = 2400 − 720 = 1680 SAR. This means a household consuming 8,000 kWh that month pays SAR 1,680.
