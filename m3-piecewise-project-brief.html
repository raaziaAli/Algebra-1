📋  Overview

Piecewise functions are everywhere in the real world — tax brackets, electricity tariffs, parking fees, delivery pricing, and speed limits all follow different rules for different intervals. In this project you will find, model, and explain a real-world situation using a piecewise function with at least 3 pieces.

Not everything in real life follows one simple rule. A taxi charges differently after the first kilometre. A phone plan changes once you hit your data limit. An electricity bill has one rate for the first 500 kWh and another after that. These are piecewise functions — and they're everywhere.

In this project, you'll take a real-world scenario and model it mathematically. You'll define the rules, set the domains, write the function notation, and build the graph. By the end, you won't just understand piecewise functions — you'll have used them to tell a mathematical story about the real world.

Work through the three parts in order: read the brief → study the exemplar → complete your own workbook.

🎯  Project Goals

By the end of this project, you will be able to:

1
Recognise real-world situations that can be modelled using piecewise functions — where different rules apply over different intervals.
2
Define domains for each piece using correct inequality notation (e.g. 0 ≤ x < 5).
3
Write a piecewise function using proper mathematical notation, with the correct rule for each domain.
4
Graph a piecewise function accurately — including open and closed endpoints, correct slopes, and labelled axes.
5
Interpret your graph in context — explaining what each piece represents in the real-world scenario.
6
Evaluate a piecewise function at specific input values and explain what the output means in context.

📌  Approved Scenarios
Electricity Tariff
Tiered billing
Saudi Electricity Company charges different rates per kWh across three consumption tiers. Model the monthly bill as a piecewise function of total kWh consumed.
Taxi / Ride Fare
Transport
A fare is calculated with a fixed base, a per-km rate, and a higher rate after a certain distance. Model the total fare as a piecewise function of km travelled.

Finance
Tax rates differ across income bands. Model the total tax owed as a piecewise function of annual income.
Mobile Data Plan
Technology

📊  Rubric

Your project is assessed across three criteria using four performance levels. Your workbook grades these automatically when you submit — your teacher reviews and finalises your level.

● Proficient (PR) ● Developing (DV) ● Emerging (EM) ● Not Assessed (NA)
1. Concepts & Procedures
Piecewise notation  ·  Graph  ·  Evaluation
Proficient
Carries out all procedures with precision and fluency. Correctly writes piecewise notation with all pieces, conditions, and curly-brace format. Accurately graphs all segments with correct open/closed endpoints, slopes, and labelled axes. Evaluates f(x) at multiple values across all pieces without errors. Makes clear connections between the algebraic rule, table of values, and graph.
Developing
Carries out procedures with some precision. Piecewise notation is mostly correct with one or two minor errors in domain boundaries or endpoint notation. Graph is largely accurate with small errors at one or two segments. Makes some connections between representations.
Emerging
Carries out procedures with limited precision. Piecewise notation attempted but domains are incomplete or incorrect. Graph has several errors in segments or endpoint dots. Evaluation of f(x) contains errors or is incomplete.
Not Assessed
Did not demonstrate knowledge or understanding of piecewise procedures. No notation attempted, no graph produced, or no evaluations completed.
2. Reasoning & Communication
Mathematical language  ·  Notation  ·  Justification
Proficient
Communicates using precise mathematical language throughout — domain, range, interval, boundary, continuity, open/closed dot — with correct symbolic notation and units. Clearly explains what each piece represents in real-world context with a detailed, organised mathematical argument. Makes valid conceptual connections and justifies all mathematical claims logically.
Developing
Uses some mathematical language and mostly correct notation. Explains the meaning of most pieces in context with units mostly present. Makes some valid arguments but justification of some claims is incomplete or partially organised.
Emerging
Uses limited mathematical language. Notation has significant errors or is missing. Explanation of real-world meaning is vague, incomplete, or missing units. Mathematical thinking is present but disorganised.
Not Assessed
Did not demonstrate mathematical language or mathematical thinking. No contextual explanation present.
3. Problem Solving & Modelling
Scenario selection  ·  Model construction  ·  Analysis
Proficient
Selects an appropriate real-world scenario and constructs a complete, accurate piecewise model with at least 3 pieces. Executes all procedures accurately. Provides in-depth written analysis explaining why piecewise modelling fits the situation, what each piece represents, and what happens at boundaries.
Developing
Selects an appropriate scenario and builds a mostly complete model. Executes procedures with some minor computational errors. Evaluates the function at most input values correctly. Provides some analysis of the modelling choice.
Emerging
Selects a scenario but the model is incomplete or has conceptual errors. Evaluation of f(x) contains errors. Analysis of the modelling choice is limited — pieces or boundaries are not explained in real-world terms.
Not Assessed
Did not provide analysis of problem solving strategies. No real-world scenario modelled, or no evaluation or analysis attempted.
